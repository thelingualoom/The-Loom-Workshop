Word Formation Processes — Reference Guide

Prefixation

A prefix is attached to the beginning of a base word, changing its meaning without changing its word class.

unhappy rewrite preview misuse disagree antiwar

Suffixation

A suffix is added to the end of a base word. Derivational suffixes often change the word class; inflectional suffixes mark grammatical categories.

teacher happiness quickly readable nationalize running

Compounding

Two or more free morphemes (independent words) are combined to form a new word. The compound can be closed, hyphenated, or open.

toothbrush blackbird sunlight bookstore heartbeat

Conversion (Zero Derivation)

A word changes its grammatical category (e.g. noun → verb) without any addition of an affix. The form stays the same; the function changes.

email google text bottle microwave

Clipping

A word is shortened by removing one or more syllables from the full form. The meaning stays the same, though register often becomes informal.

ad gym phone flu lab fridge

Blending

Parts of two or more words are merged into a single new word (portmanteau). Typically the beginning of one word is joined with the end of another.

brunch smog blog sitcom hangry glamping

Acronym & Initialism

An acronym is formed from initial letters of a phrase and pronounced as a word (NASA). An initialism is spelled out letter by letter (FBI).

NASA laser radar scuba wifi

Back-formation

A shorter word is created by removing an affix from a longer existing word — the reverse of the usual affixation process.

edit babysit televise donate enthuse sculpt

Reduplication

A word or part of a word is repeated, either exactly (full reduplication) or with a vowel/consonant change (partial reduplication).

bye-bye so-so night-night chit-chat zigzag flip-flop

Onomatopoeia

A word is created to phonetically imitate or suggest the sound it describes. The word's pronunciation echoes its meaning.

buzz hiss bang splash sizzle murmur

Borrowing (Loanword)

A word is adopted from another language, sometimes with minor phonological or orthographic adaptation. English has borrowed extensively from French, Latin, Greek, Arabic, and many others.

ballet pizza algebra tsunami kindergarten yoga

Coinage / Neologism

An entirely new word is invented, not derived from any existing word or morpheme. Often associated with brand names or technological inventions.

kodak xerox aspirin nylon googol
